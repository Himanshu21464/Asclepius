Verify a bundle without trusting us.

An Asclepius evidence bundle is a USTAR .tar file. The verifier needs only tar, openssl, jq, and sha256sum — all already on every regulator's laptop. No network. No vendor library. No "trust us." Five steps.

01

Extract the tar.

Every bundle is a flat USTAR archive. There are no symlinks, no nested directories, no compression — just files. tar xf is enough.

~ $ tar xf evidence-2026-04.tar ~ $ ls ground_truth.jsonl ledger_excerpt.jsonl manifest.json manifest.sig metrics.json overrides.jsonl public_key drift.json
What you have now. Nine files. manifest.json is the index; manifest.sig is its 64-byte Ed25519 signature; public_key is the 32-byte verification key. The other six are the data the manifest indexes.
02

Authenticate the manifest.

Verify that the bytes of manifest.json were signed by the operator's key. If the signature doesn't match, you know nothing about the rest of the bundle and you should stop.

# convert the raw 32-byte public key to PEM ~ $ { printf '\\x30\\x2a\\x30\\x05\\x06\\x03\\x2b\\x65\\x70\\x03\\x21\\x00'; cat public_key; } | openssl base64 \\ | sed -e '1i-----BEGIN PUBLIC KEY-----' \\ -e '$a-----END PUBLIC KEY-----' > pk.pem ~ $ openssl pkeyutl -verify \\ -pubin -inkey pk.pem \\ -sigfile manifest.sig \\ -in manifest.json Signature Verified Successfully
Why this works offline. Ed25519 verification is purely arithmetic over Curve25519. openssl needs no network, no certificate authority, no clock. The public key was published when the system was deployed.
03

Match every file to its hash.

The manifest declares a SHA-256 for each data file. Re-hash the files you actually have and confirm. This is what catches tampering.

~ $ jq -r '.files | to_entries[] | "\(.value) \(.key)"' \\ manifest.json | sha256sum -c ledger_excerpt.jsonl: OK metrics.json: OK drift.json: OK overrides.jsonl: OK ground_truth.jsonl: OK
A single FAILED here means the bundle has been altered after signing. Pair it with step 02 — both must pass.
04

Walk the ledger excerpt.

The ledger entries are JSON-Lines. Each line carries its own seq, prev_hash, payload_hash, and signature. Confirm the chain — every prev_hash equals the previous entry's entry_hash.

~ $ jq -c '. | {seq, ts, event_type, prev_hash}' \\ ledger_excerpt.jsonl | head -3 {"seq":1,"ts":"2026-04-01T08:14:02Z","event_type":"inference.committed","prev_hash":"0000…00"} {"seq":2,"event_type":"evaluation.override","prev_hash":"3f9c1ad8…0e7d21"} {"seq":3,"event_type":"evaluation.ground_truth","prev_hash":"8e510bf4…21b04a"} ~ $ # or ask asclepius itself to verify the excerpt: ~ $ asclepius evidence verify evidence-2026-04.tar OK
What the chain proves. If the operator silently dropped row N, the prev_hash on row N+1 would no longer match. If they edited row N's body, the recomputed payload_hash would differ from the one in the header, and that one was signed.
05

Read the metrics.

You now know the bytes are authentic and the chain is whole. The actual evidence is in metrics.json + drift.json. Read them as a regulator would.

~ $ jq '.[] | {model, n_inferences, override_rate, accuracy}' \\ metrics.json {"model":"scribe@v3","n_inferences":148302,"override_rate":0.041,"accuracy":0.972} {"model":"diag@v2", "n_inferences":8917, "override_rate":0.213,"accuracy":0.881} ~ $ jq '.[] | select(.severity != "none")' drift.json {"feature":"confidence","psi":0.243,"severity":"minor","computed_at":"2026-04-25T17:22Z"}
That is the entirety of the verifier protocol. Five commands. No vendor SDK. No network. No "trust the dashboard." A regulator with a laptop and the public key gets cryptographic certainty about what the deployed system did, and a structured-prose summary of what it learned.

For programmatic verification from your own code, link libasclepius and call EvaluationHarness::verify_bundle(path). Same checks, same result. Source: src/evaluation/bundle.cpp.
