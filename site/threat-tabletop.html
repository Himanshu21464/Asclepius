What the chain actually catches.

Threat models are abstract. Tabletop exercises are not. Below, five concrete adversarial scenarios — each with the attacker's capability, the narrative, what the substrate observes, the expected time-to-detect, the runbook to follow, and the residual risk after response. Run them in your own SOC. Tell us what we missed.

TT-01 · insider re-write A1 · log-tampering insider window: minutes

An insider tries to re-write a row.

An employee with database credentials wants to silently change the output of inference seq=874 after a malpractice complaint. They have full SQLite access; they connect, locate the row, and update its output_hash, leaving the rest unchanged. They restart the runtime to clear caches. They go home.

The next nightly verifier run hashes seq=873 and computes the expected prev_hash for seq=874. The stored prev_hash on seq=874 still matches — it was not edited. But the row's own signed body now hashes to a different value than the one seq=875's prev_hash embeds. The chain breaks at 875.

what the substrate sees

  • asclepius_ledger_chain_broken_total increments at next verify
  • log: chain.broken at seq=875
  • compare.html highlights seq=874 amber if two bundles span the edit
  • signature on seq=874 still verifies — body matches sig — but the chain link fails

response

  1. page · runbook PB-01
  2. flip runtime.read_only=true
  3. snapshot WAL · diff against last verified backup
  4. identify modified row · escalate to security oncall
  5. publish operator advisory · disclose timeline

residual risk

  • between insider edit and next verifier run, downstream consumers may have read the modified value
  • mitigation: nightly is the floor; high-stakes deployments verify hourly
  • retroactive backups are the recovery path · the chain is the alarm
detect: ≤ 24h (nightly) / ≤ 1h (hourly verify) response runbook: PB-01 threat-model adversary: A1
TT-02 · retroactive consent forgery A2 · consent fabricator window: seconds

Forging a consent after the fact.

A patient files a complaint that an AI scribe was used during a visit for which they never granted consent. The clinic, panicking, asks an ops engineer to "back-date" a consent.granted entry to cover the encounter. The engineer attempts to insert a row with seq in the past.

The runtime refuses: seq is monotonic and gaps are forbidden by spec. If they instead append a fresh entry at the current tail with a back-dated ts, the entry's recorded-at time is preserved separately by the verifier and the discrepancy is visible. Either way, the forgery surfaces.

what the substrate sees

  • insert at past seq → runtime refuses · log: ledger.append.rejected
  • append-with-backdated-ts → verifier flags ts vs recorded_at divergence
  • auditor's compare.html bundle diff catches a "new" row inserted at a sequence already in the prior bundle

response

  1. privacy-team incident · runbook PB-05 (inverse)
  2. preserve the forgery attempt as evidence · do not delete
  3. publish patient-facing acknowledgement · 24 h SLA
  4. conduct breach-notification analysis (HIPAA / state law)

residual risk

  • between encounter and forgery, a brief window where audits would have shown "no consent on file"
  • this is not a substrate failure — it is the substrate doing exactly what it should
  • training: ops engineers must understand that the chain is not negotiable
detect: instant (refused) or next compare run (revealed) response runbook: PB-05 threat-model adversary: A2
TT-03 · silent model-version swap A3 · vendor-side substitution window: until next inference

The vendor swaps the model mid-deployment.

An ambient-scribe vendor ships a "free" upgrade overnight. The container's scribe-v3 tag now points to a different set of weights with subtly different behaviour. The vendor does not announce it. The clinical team starts to see slightly different note structures, but it is gradual and easy to attribute to "the model learning."

The substrate computes weights_sha256 on every inference.completed entry. The hash changes the moment the new weights load. The chain still verifies — the new weights are what produced the output — but the FDA-cleared artefact is no longer the deployed artefact. The substrate has the receipts.

what the substrate sees

  • new weights_sha256 appears in asclepius_inferences_total labels
  • alert: weights_sha256 not in cleared-artefact allowlist
  • OTel span asclepius.inference attribute changes; trace tools surface it
  • FDA post-market dossier for next reporting period flags the discrepancy

response

  1. flip the vendor integration to read_only until clarified
  2. contact vendor; request weights_sha256 reconciliation
  3. file PCCP-deviation memo if the swap is unauthorised
  4. file safety report with FDA if patient-facing impact suspected
  5. append model.deviation entry to the chain

residual risk

  • any inferences before detection used uncleared artefact · scope by hash transition seq
  • mitigation: pin weights_sha256 at deploy time; refuse on mismatch
  • if pinning is not feasible, the substrate at minimum records the truth
detect: first inference after swap response runbook: PB-04 (drift adjacent) · custom PCCP memo threat-model adversary: A3
TT-04 · drift gaslighting A4 · adversarial input distribution window: hours–days

Gaslighting the drift monitor.

An attacker (or a misbehaving upstream pipeline) feeds the model a slowly-shifting distribution of inputs, intending to gradually move the population such that drift detection rolls forward without ever crossing a threshold. The goal is to land the model in a regime where its calibration has degraded but the substrate's PSI never breached 0.25.

The substrate watches three statistics — PSI on binned features, KS on continuous, EMD on ordinal — and aggregates over windows of 1d, 7d, and 30d. A creep across the 7d window will eventually breach the 30d window, and vice versa. Even when no single threshold fires, the ledger's drift snapshots make the gradual transition visible retrospectively in any bundle export.

what the substrate sees

  • PSI/KS/EMD trends across windows captured every drift snapshot
  • multi-window divergence makes "hide the drift" require shifting all three at once — much harder
  • calibration loss surfaces in QA harness reports independent of drift
  • conformance suite L2 fails on cumulative shift even if instantaneous is fine

response

  1. runbook PB-04
  2. investigate input pipeline · was this organic shift or upstream change?
  3. downgrade purpose to advisory_only while investigating
  4. append drift.acknowledged with rationale
  5. schedule re-evaluation; consider model retraining

residual risk

  • some misclassifications between threshold breach and acknowledgement are unavoidable
  • multi-window detection is harder to evade but not impossible
  • backstop: calibration loss in QA reports is an orthogonal signal
detect: hours (multi-window) to days (calibration QA) response runbook: PB-04 threat-model adversary: A4
TT-05 · key leak (HSM bypass) A5 · privileged-credential attacker window: minutes (with tooling)

The signing key leaks.

An attacker exfiltrates the active Ed25519 private key — perhaps via a sidechannel against an HSM that wasn't quite the HSM it claimed to be, or via a misconfigured role on a cloud key-management service. They now have the ability to sign arbitrary entries that will verify under the deployed pubkey.

They cannot, however, append entries to the substrate's ledger without also writing them to the database, because every entry needs a valid prev_hash. If they fork their own ledger and try to substitute it, downstream subscribers and the public Merkle-root feed catch the divergence. The chain is gossipable.

what the substrate sees

  • local ledger does not know the leak occurred — until divergence is observed
  • downstream subscriber computes a different tail_root than what the local runtime publishes
  • HSM operation log shows unexplained sign calls (if HSM is honest)
  • auditor running compare.html sees ghost entries that don't exist in local backups

response

  1. declare incident · runbook PB-03
  2. emergency rotate · publish key.revoked entry
  3. publish revocation to /.well-known/security.txt
  4. do not re-sign past entries · the chain is forensic
  5. file CVE within 72h
  6. auditors: re-pull bundles, verify with new pubkey, flag everything between LKG and revocation as under review

residual risk

  • any forged entries the attacker successfully gossiped to a third party retain a valid sig forever — see ADR-001
  • the chain forensic property — "compromised entries stay signed by compromised key" — is a feature
  • recovery is a process, not a button
detect: gossip / divergence within hours · HSM log if available response runbook: PB-03 threat-model adversary: A5

What tabletop exercises are for.

Reading a threat model tells you what could go wrong. Walking through these exercises tells you what your team is actually prepared to do. Run them at the substrate layer; run them in your SOC; run them with your legal team. If a step in any of these doesn't survive "okay, you, do it now," you have a planning problem, not a runtime problem.

These five are the obvious ones. We will add new exercises as the ecosystem develops new attack patterns — contribute one if your team finds something we haven't covered.
