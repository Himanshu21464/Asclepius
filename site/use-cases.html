§01 · scribe · diagnostic · agentic

I would integrate this for…

Asclepius is a substrate, not a product — so the question is never does it work, it is what does it look like once you have wrapped your model in it. Three concrete deployments, each with the policy chain that runs, the boundary the model sees, the consent flow that gates it, and the bundle a regulator gets back.

§01 an ambient scribe microphone in the room → SOAP note in the EHR. The dictation is PHI; the note is a clinical artefact. Where exactly does the audit live? §02 a diagnostic second-read CT chest comes back; an LLM-assisted system flags possible PE. What does the radiologist see, and what does the trail look like six months later when the case goes to peer review? §03 an agentic action a triage agent that drafts orders. The model proposes, the substrate disposes — every consent boundary and every action is enforceable, not advisory.
§01 · clinic encounter · 19 minutes · two participants

An ambient scribe.

A family-medicine clinician sees a patient for shortness of breath. A microphone in the corner streams the conversation to a hosted scribe model, which returns a structured SOAP note that lands in the EHR. This is the most common clinical-AI deployment in the world right now.

It is also the deployment where the substrate matters most quietly. The model never sees identifiers — it sees a transcript scrubbed of names, MRNs, dates of birth, and addresses. The note that lands in the EHR is verifiable: every sentence the model generated is signed and bound to the audio span it was derived from. If a billing dispute arises in 2027, the operator does not argue about what the model wrote — they replay the bundle.

What this buys you. The note in the EHR is not a black box. It is one signed row in a chain whose root the operator publishes weekly. A clinician can show a patient exactly what the model heard and exactly what it wrote; a regulator can replay any encounter; a peer reviewer can diff the SOAP against the transcript without ever asking the vendor. Asclepius did not write the note — it made the note testifiable.

§02 · radiology · CT chest · 1.2 s decision window

A diagnostic second-read.

A radiologist reads a CT pulmonary angiogram. An LLM-assisted system reviews the same scan and produces a structured suggestion: "high probability segmental PE, left lower lobe, 87% confidence, four supporting ROIs." The radiologist concurs, dissents, or asks a follow-up. Six months later, the case goes to peer review.

The substrate question is not was the model right. It is can we prove what the model said, what the radiologist saw, and when. Asclepius signs the suggestion, signs the radiologist's decision, signs the order they placed (or did not), and binds the three together with a Merkle root the operator publishes nightly. The chain is the testimony.

peer-review replay (six months later)

  1. auditor exports the encounter — asclepius bundle export enc:…
  2. verifier rebuilds the Merkle root locally — verify_bundle() → ✓

  5. drift histogram shows the case lay inside the calibrated distribution

What this buys you. When the case goes to peer review, the question "would a reasonable radiologist have ordered heparin?" becomes answerable from the chain alone — not from screenshots, not from EHR reconstruction, not from a vendor's word. The model is a participant whose contribution is recorded, not a deus ex machina whose reasoning evaporates.

§03 · ED triage · agent draft · attending sign-off

An agentic action.

The hardest case. An ED triage agent ingests the chief complaint, vitals, and chart context, and proposes actions: a chest x-ray, a troponin, a 12-lead, a low-acuity room. Three of the four are routine; one of them is a decision the attending needs to ratify. The model is no longer suggesting — it is acting through a delegate.

The substrate is no longer optional. Every proposed action runs through an ActionFilter that lookups the action in a blast-radius table; any action above the configured threshold is staged, not committed, and held until an attending signs. The signature, the staging, the commit, and the underlying model output are four separate signed rows — and the system refuses to commit if any one of them is missing.

two-hop signature chain — model proposes, human disposes, both signed

blast-radius table (excerpt)

  • order.cxr — auto-commit · low blast radius
  • order.troponin — auto-commit · low
  • order.heparin.continuous — requires attending
  • order.discharge — requires attending + nursing read-back
  • contact.next-of-kin — never agentic; human-only
  • scope.escalate — never agentic; explicit policy change

What this buys you. Agentic systems are normally accountability black holes: "the model did it" is not a defense in court, but neither is "the human approved something they did not understand." The substrate forces every action to be either auto-commit-with-low-blast-radius or human-signed-after-read-back, and refuses to honour anything in between. The chain is what makes the difference enforceable. The agent is fast. The substrate is answerable.

None of these are features.

The same Merkle ledger, the same policy-chain primitive, the same consent registry, the same drift monitor, the same evidence-bundle export — applied to three different shapes of clinical-AI deployment. Asclepius is not a scribe, not a diagnostic aid, not an agent. It is the layer that makes any of those a thing a hospital can defend.

If your deployment does not look like one of these three, it almost certainly decomposes into a combination. Read the spec; bring the substrate up against your shape; tell us where it bends.
