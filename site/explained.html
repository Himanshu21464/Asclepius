Asclepius, explained.

The rest of this site assumes you already speak the vocabulary of trust substrates, Merkle ledgers, and policy chains. This page does not.

It is for hospital administrators, doctors who don't build AI, journalists, compliance officers, and curious developers who want the shape of the thing before the spec. Read it once and the rest of the site stops being cryptic.

§01 in three sentences

What this is.

Asclepius is an open-source piece of software that sits between any AI tool used in a hospital and the rest of the hospital. Every time the AI sees something or says something, Asclepius writes down a tamper-evident record of what happened. Months or years later, a regulator can look at that record and verify it without trusting the hospital, the AI vendor, or us.

That's the whole product. Everything else on this site is detail.

§02 the problem

The problem.

Hospitals are already running clinical AI. Ambient scribes that listen to visits and write the SOAP note. Diagnostic systems that suggest a finding on a chest X-ray. Triage models that score who in the waiting room is sickest. Billing models that decide whether a stay was medically necessary. This is not a forecast — it is current production traffic, in millions of encounters per week.

When something goes wrong with one of these tools — a missed diagnosis, a denied claim, a recorded conversation that should not have been recorded, a recommendation that quietly drifted off the population it was trained on — the hospital usually cannot prove what happened. There is no canonical record of what the AI saw, what it said, who approved it, which safety filters ran, or whether the model was still behaving the way it did at deployment.

Regulators have noticed. The FDA, ONC, and state attorneys general are starting to demand that proof. AI vendors, asked to provide it, mostly say trust us. That gap — between what's being asked and what anyone can actually produce — is what Asclepius exists to close.

concrete Q1 2026 alone: a $556M Medicare-Advantage AI settlement, 100,000+ ambient-scribe encounters recorded without consent at a single health system, and a denial model whose decisions were reportedly reversed on appeal about 90% of the time. None of the three had an audit trail anyone could verify.

§03 what asclepius does

What Asclepius does.

Picture the AI tool as a black box. Today, the hospital sends inputs into the box and gets outputs back, and that's most of what's recorded. Asclepius is a thin wrapper that you install around that box. Every interaction passes through the wrapper on the way in and on the way out.

For every interaction the wrapper writes one line into a ledger. The line records:

  • The input — fingerprinted (hashed), never the raw protected health information itself.
  • The output — what the AI said, also fingerprinted.
  • The actor — which clinician, on which device, in which tenant.
  • The privacy filters that ran — and whether any of them blocked the call.
  • The clinician's response — accepted, edited, or overridden.
  • The model's behavior over time — whether its outputs are starting to drift away from the distribution it was tested on.
  • Consent — did this patient agree to be processed by this kind of AI for this purpose.

Each line is cryptographically signed and chained to the previous line, the way a blockchain chains its blocks — but with no coins, no consensus, no network. It's just math on a file. Change one byte anywhere in the history and the chain breaks visibly.

Hand that file to a regulator and they can verify it on a laptop, offline, with a public key. They don't need to trust the hospital. They don't need to trust the AI vendor. They don't need to trust us. The bytes carry the proof.

§04 who it is for

Who it's for.

Five audiences, in roughly the order they tend to find us:

  • Hospitals deploying clinical AI — health systems that have one or more AI tools in production and a CISO, compliance officer, or chief medical informatics officer who has been asked, "can you prove this is safe?"
  • AI vendors who have to prove safety — scribe companies, radiology AI firms, decision-support startups that need an audit story they did not have to invent themselves.
  • Regulators — FDA reviewers, state AGs, accreditation bodies. The receiving end of an evidence bundle. They run our verifier, not our runtime.
  • Security and compliance teams — the people who would rather not write yet another bespoke audit pipeline in Python.
  • Researchers — academics studying real-world AI behavior, drift, and override patterns, who currently have no standard substrate to study.

Patients are the ultimate beneficiaries, but they are not the ones installing the software. We design defaults for the patient; we ship the tool to the operator.

§05 why this layer

Why it has to exist at this layer.

A reasonable question: why isn't this already built? Three groups could plausibly ship it, and none of them will.

Model providers won't ship this. Their incentive is to be trusted by reputation, not by proof. A vendor offering cryptographic provenance against itself is offering a stick to be hit with. The strongest of them might publish model cards and uptime dashboards; none of them will publish a tamper-evident chain that lets a regulator audit them line-by-line.

EHR vendors won't ship this. For Epic, Cerner, athena, this is a compliance problem two layers down — interesting in slide decks, never on the roadmap. The same institutional gravity that keeps EHR APIs slow keeps AI auditing out of the EHR.

Hospitals can't build this. The cryptography is specialized. The drift statistics are specialized. The consent model is specialized. The bundle format is specialized. Doing it once in one health system is a six-figure infrastructure project; doing it in a thousand health systems is a tragedy.

It belongs at the substrate layer — beneath the AI tool, beneath the EHR, beneath any one vendor's interest. The same layer Linux occupies for an operating system, or Postgres for a database. Not glamorous. Not optional. The Linux kernel for clinical AI.

Apache 2.0. Vendor-neutral. No SaaS hooks, no telemetry, no call-home. If we did this right, in five years it disappears into the stack and no one talks about it anymore — the way no one talks about TLS.

§06 what you actually get

What you actually get.

Concretely, six things. Each is a feature you would otherwise build by hand.

audit

Signed Merkle audit chain

An append-only log of every inference, override, and consent event. Each row is hashed and linked to the row before it; the whole chain is signed. Tampering is mathematically detectable.

consent

Consent registry with restart replay

Patient-by-patient, purpose-by-purpose record of who agreed to what. The runtime refuses to call the AI when consent is missing — and the refusal itself is logged.

privacy

PHI-scrubbing policy chain

Composable filters that run before the AI ever sees the input. Strip identifiers, validate shape, cap length, deny clinical actions outside a list. Hospitals add their own.

drift

Drift detection (PSI / KS / EMD)

Statistical monitors that watch the live distribution of inputs and outputs against the deployment baseline. Crossover thresholds raise an alert before quality degrades visibly.

human

Clinician override capture

Every time a clinician edits or rejects an AI output, the override is recorded with reason. This is the ground truth signal regulators and product teams both want.

export

Evidence-bundle export

One command produces a single signed archive — the chain excerpt, the keys, the manifest, the verifier — that a regulator can open and verify offline, without contacting your servers.

§07 what it is not

What it is not.

Equally important. People keep arriving expecting one of these and we'd rather you save the trip:

  • an AI scribe
  • a diagnostic model
  • an MLOps platform
  • an EHR plugin
  • a SaaS product
  • a model itself
  • a replacement for clinical judgment
  • a governance committee
  • a privacy lawyer
  • a data warehouse

Asclepius does not generate notes, suggest diagnoses, train models, host weights, or decide policy. It records, signs, and verifies what other things did. It is the layer that makes the rest of the stack auditable. If you need any of the items above, you need them on top of Asclepius, not instead of it.

§08 a 30-second use case

A 30-second use case.

The shortest concrete example we can give. Imagine a regional health system running an ambient-scribe AI on a GPU node in their datacenter. The scribe listens during primary-care visits, drafts a SOAP note, the clinician edits it, and the final version goes into the EHR. Hundreds of encounters per day per site.

  1. The IT team installs Asclepius as a sidecar on the GPU node. The scribe's existing call to the model now goes through the wrapper. No application code changes; one config flag.

  2. For each encounter, Asclepius records: a hash of the audio transcript, a hash of the draft note, which PHI-scrub policies ran, whether the clinician edited or accepted, and how long inference took.

  3. The scribe vendor pushes a new model version. Asclepius starts comparing the live input distribution to the baseline; three weeks in, it flags a crossover — accents from a new clinic the scribe wasn't trained on. The CMIO sees the alert before anyone files a complaint.

  4. Three months later, a regulator opens an inquiry: show every diagnosis suggestion this AI made for patient X during March. The compliance team runs one command:

$ asclepius ledger range-by-patient pat:9f1a-...-c20e --month 2026-03 --bundle out.tar
  ↳ wrote out.tar (verified · 217 entries · seq 0x01A4F→0x01B22 · ed25519 a1f2…74e6)

The regulator receives out.tar. They run the included verifier on a laptop, offline. The verifier walks the chain, checks every signature, and reports either chain intact or broken at seq N. There is no way for the hospital to have edited the record after the fact without the verifier saying so.

That is the entire customer experience. The day-to-day cost was a small percentage of GPU time. The day-it-mattered value was an audit story that would otherwise have cost six figures and three months of consultants to assemble.
