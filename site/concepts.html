Asclepius, in concept.

/explained answers what is this? in plain words. /architecture answers how is it built? in spec-grade detail. This page sits between them: the vocabulary the rest of the site assumes, with the substrate intuitions that make the spec readable.

It is for engineers, regulators, security architects, and clinical informaticists who want the why each piece is shaped this way before reading SPEC.md line-for-line. Eight short sections; thirty minutes end-to-end.

§01 trust substrate, defined

What "trust substrate" actually means.

The word substrate is borrowed from infrastructure. In a Kubernetes cluster, the compute substrate is the layer that sits between containers and machines and proves things about how they were scheduled. In TLS, the transport substrate sits between two sockets and proves things about how they exchanged bytes. In Postgres, the storage substrate sits between a query and a disk and proves things about commit ordering and durability.

A trust substrate is the same shape, applied to clinical AI. It sits between a clinical AI tool and the rest of a hospital's systems and proves things about how they interacted. Not what the AI should have said, not whether the diagnosis was correct — those are model-quality questions. A substrate has no opinion on those. It records, in tamper-evident form, what actually happened, and lets a third party verify that record without trusting any of the participants.

Three properties together earn the word:

  • Thinness. The substrate is small relative to the systems on either side of it. Asclepius is roughly twelve thousand lines of C++; a clinical AI model is millions of weights and ten thousand lines of serving code. The substrate is not a competitor to either; it would not fit.
  • Vendor-neutrality. The substrate has no preference among the systems above it. Wrap an Epic-installed scribe, an open-source classifier, an in-house triage model — the substrate behaves the same way. Apache 2.0; no SaaS hooks.
  • Provability. Every claim the substrate makes — about consent, about drift, about which policy ran — reduces to bytes that a third party can re-check. The substrate does not say trust me. It hands you the math.

analogy The same way Linux does not care which application sits on top of read() and write() — it just guarantees the syscall semantics — Asclepius does not care which model sits on top of begin_inference(). It just guarantees the ledger semantics.

§02 the audit chain

The audit chain.

Every interaction the substrate sees becomes one row in an append-only log. The log is a Merkle chain — a fancy phrase for a simple discipline: each row carries the hash of the row before it, and the whole row (including that link) is signed. 

─── one row of the chain ───────────────────────
seq           = 42
timestamp     = 2026-04-26T17:22:25.301Z
event_type    = inference.committed
actor         = clinician:dr.demo
payload_hash  = blake2b256(// canonical event body)
prev_hash     = blake2b256(row #41)   // the link
signature     = ed25519(payload_hash || prev_hash)

That structure has two consequences worth memorizing.

Tampering is mathematically detectable. Edit one byte at seq 17 and the hash of seq 17 changes. That breaks the prev_hash link in seq 18, which breaks 19, which breaks every row up to the head. A verifier walking the chain sees the break at exactly seq 17 — the row whose recomputed hash no longer matches what the next row references.

Signing makes the chain authoring-evident, not just ordering-evident. The Ed25519 signature binds each row to a specific keypair. Anyone holding the public key can verify the signature; only the holder of the private key could have produced it. So a regulator with the public key can confirm both this is the order in which events happened and they were authored by the runtime that holds this key.

There is no blockchain in the cryptocurrency sense — no consensus, no network, no miners, no coins. The chain is a single SQLite file, written by one process, signed with one key. The cryptography is the part that scales; the operations are deliberately small.

why ed25519 + blake2b Both are libsodium-stock primitives — a single-vendor dependency reduces the audit surface. Ed25519 signs in ~50 µs per row on commodity silicon; BLAKE2b-256 hashes at >1 GB/s. The substrate adds <200 µs to a model call that takes hundreds of milliseconds, so its overhead is invisible in production but visible in /benchmarks.

Inclusion proofs.

For a regulator who only cares about one entry — a single diagnosis suggestion on a specific patient on a specific day — the substrate can produce an inclusion proof: a compact witness that this seq belongs to the published chain whose head hash you have on file. The witness is verifiable offline against the head alone; the regulator does not need to download the whole chain. (See Ledger::inclusion_proof(seq) in the API.)

§03 consent as state

Consent as state, not just a checkbox.

Most clinical-AI consent today is a row in a database that says patient signed a form on date X. It records that consent was, at one moment, given. It does not record that consent is, at the moment of the AI call, still active, scoped to this purpose, and not revoked.

Asclepius treats consent as live runtime state. A ConsentToken carries the patient, the granted purposes, the grant timestamp, the absolute expiry, and — when revocation has happened — a revoked_at wall-clock instant. Every grant, extension, expiry, and revocation appends a ledger entry; the in-memory registry is reconstructable from the chain alone. The audit chain is the source of truth for consent.

checkbox
"the patient signed a form once"
state
"this AI call, for this patient, for this purpose, is permitted right now — and the runtime can prove it from the chain"
checkbox
revocation is "we put a flag in the EHR"
state
revocation appends consent.revoked; subsequent inferences refuse to run; the refusal itself is logged
checkbox
scope is loose — "agreed to AI"
state
scope is one of eight codified Purpose values; adding a new one is a governance event, not a string change

A permits(patient, purpose) check is on the inference hot path. If consent is missing, the inference does not run. This is the property that distinguishes a substrate from a logger: the substrate refuses the call. A logger only records that the call happened.

recovery If the in-memory registry is wiped — restart, crash, migration — the runtime replays consent state from the ledger on startup (ingest() over tail_by_event_type). The operator never needs to back up consent state separately; the chain is the backup.

Consent in the India profile.

Round 90 added three substrate primitives that layer over ConsentRegistry without changing its API. The kernel does not pick a jurisdiction; the India profile is one optional configuration of the same substrate.

  • ConsentArtefact — the JSON-serialisable wire format the kernel exchanges with HIPs, HIUs, and the ABDM Gateway. artefact_from_token / token_from_artefact bidirectionally map between the in-memory token and the externalised artefact.
  • FamilyGraph — proxy → subject edges with a four-value FamilyRelation enum. A token issued by a proxy is honoured iff a valid edge exists. Edge writes fire an observer so the audit ledger reconstructs the graph on replay.
  • EmergencyOverride — DPDP § 7 deferred-consent flow. activate() records the break-glass; backfill() files the post-hoc evidence; is_overdue() surfaces tokens whose configurable mandatory window (default 72h) has lapsed.

See also: /india for the full profile, ADR-013 for the rationale, api.html → consent.hpp for the public surface.

§04 drift, in three numbers

Drift, in three numbers.

A clinical AI deployed on Tuesday is not necessarily the same clinical AI on Friday. The model's weights have not changed — but the population it sees has. New clinic, new accents, a flu wave, a different referral pattern. The model's outputs start to drift away from the distribution it was tested on. By the time the outputs are visibly worse, weeks of degraded care have already landed.

The substrate detects drift before that happens, by comparing the live distribution of inputs and outputs against the deployment baseline. Three statistics, run in parallel — each catches what the others miss.

psi

Population Stability Index

Sum of (pi − qi) · ln(pi / qi) over bins. Catches mass moving between bins of a binned distribution. Industry default for tabular drift; well-understood thresholds: < 0.10 stable, 0.10–0.25 minor, > 0.25 significant.

ks

Kolmogorov-Smirnov

Maximum gap between two empirical CDFs. Catches shape changes that PSI smooths over — a bimodal split, a tail-heavy turn. Distribution-free.

emd

Earth Mover's Distance

The "work" needed to move mass from one distribution to another. Catches gradual translation — values drifting up or down across the whole range — that neither PSI nor KS handles cleanly.

Each is computed per registered feature — input length, output length, latency, an extracted score. DriftMonitor::report() returns severity (none / minor / moderate / severe) per feature; any_severe() is the cheap predicate behind a page-the-on-call alert. most_drifted_feature() answers the obvious next question: which one.

The threshold lives at DriftSeverity::classify(psi) and is exposed for tests and calibration — operators tune by feature, not by global knob.

not a diagnostic Drift detection answers "is the input distribution moving?", not "is the model still correct?". A clean drift report is necessary but not sufficient. Pair drift with override capture and ground-truth attachment for a complete behavioral picture.

§05 the policy chain

The policy chain.

Every input the model is about to see, and every output it just produced, passes through a chain of small, composable filters. Each filter returns a verdict: allow, modify, or block. The first block wins; modify rewrites the payload; allow falls through. 

─── input flow ─────────────────────────────────
caller payload
   → phi_scrubber            → [REDACTED] for names, MRNs, SSNs
   → schema_validator        → JSON Schema subset, BLOCK on violation
   → length_limit(8k, 8k)    → guard against runaway tokens
   → model_call(payload)
   → clinical_action_filter  → BLOCK orders / Rx / messages outside allowlist
   → caller receives output

Four built-ins ship with the runtime. Hospitals add their own — the chain is mutable by name (policies().push, remove, has) and every mutation appends a policy.config_change ledger entry. The configuration of the chain is itself audited.

  • PHI scrubber. Conservative regex + dictionary detector for names, MRNs, SSNs, phone numbers, addresses, DOBs. Decision: MODIFY, replacing detected PHI with [REDACTED]. Favors false positives — better to over-redact than to leak.
  • Schema validator. JSON Schema subset (type / required / enum / minimum / maximum / minLength / maxLength). Decision: BLOCK. Used to enforce shape on agentic outputs that emit structured calls.
  • Length limit. Input + output max lengths. Decision: BLOCK. Cheap; first line of defense against runaway inference cost.
  • Clinical action filter. Allowlist for clinical action codes when wrapping agentic tools that emit orders or prescriptions. Decision: BLOCK on any non-allowlisted action.

Per-policy timing.

Each policy contributes its own latency to the inference budget. The metric registry tracks per-policy histograms; MetricRegistry::histogram_count(name) and histogram_sum(name) give the average; has_histogram(name) distinguishes "never registered" from "zero observations" on a dashboard. When a hospital adds a custom policy that runs slow, the policy is named in the dashboard — not buried inside a totalizer.

§06 offline-verifiable

Why offline-verifiable matters.

The whole substrate is shaped around one operational scenario: a regulator opens an inquiry, asks for evidence, and verifies it without contacting the hospital, the AI vendor, or us. The verifier runs on a laptop, air-gapped, off a USB stick.

That constraint is load-bearing. It rules out anything that depends on a phone-home check, a cloud token-exchange, a vendor license server. It rules out database-side trust ("our query returned X, you'll have to take our word for it"). It rules out opaque binary formats. The output of asclepius evidence bundle is a USTAR tar with a manifest, the relevant ledger excerpt, the public verification key, and a cross-platform verifier binary. Open it on any laptop with a working clock, run the verifier, get one of two answers: chain intact or broken at seq N.

  • Air-gapped audits. A regulator working in a sensitive jurisdiction, or a hospital security team unwilling to expose internal infrastructure, can verify in a SCIF with no network. The bundle and the verifier are the whole dependency surface.
  • Regulator independence. The regulator does not run our software in production. They run our verifier post-hoc. The trust boundary is the public key; everything else is bytes.
  • Vendor-neutral evidence. Bundles emitted by Asclepius v0.4 verify under v0.4, v0.5, v1.0. The format is frozen at v1.0 onward and bundles continue to verify with the same major version forever (see the compatibility guarantees on /changelog).

contrast An EHR-vendor "AI audit" feature ships you a dashboard you log into. A SaaS "model observability" platform ships you an invoice. Asclepius ships you a tar file. Bytes carry the proof; nothing else has to be online for the audit to clear.

§07 adjacent things

Adjacent things this is not.

The clinical-AI tooling landscape is busy and the labels overlap. Asclepius shares vocabulary with several adjacent product categories without being any of them. If you arrived expecting one of these, the project you want is on the right of the line.

  • not an MLOps platform Weights & Biases, MLflow, Comet — they track experiments and training runs. Asclepius tracks production interactions. The substrate sits below an MLOps platform, not next to it.
  • not a model-observability dashboard Arize, WhyLabs, Fiddler — beautiful UIs over inference logs you ship them. They are the consumers of an audit substrate; Asclepius is the producer. Run their dashboard; have them ingest your ledger.
  • not an EHR plugin Epic, Cerner, athena — the substrate doesn't replace a chart, doesn't normalize FHIR, doesn't do CDS hooks. It is FHIR-aware in the narrow sense that EncounterScope can extract Patient + Encounter references; that's it.
  • not an AI scribe Abridge, Nuance DAX, Suki — they generate notes. Asclepius wraps them. The scribe sits above; the substrate sits below.
  • not a diagnostic model Aidoc, Viz.ai, Tempus — they suggest diagnoses. Asclepius records what they suggested, what the clinician did with the suggestion, whether the model was drifting at the time.
  • not a governance committee The substrate produces evidence. People — CMIOs, ethics boards, regulators — make decisions. Tooling does not replace governance; it gives governance something to point at.
  • not a privacy lawyer HIPAA, GDPR, the EU AI Act — the substrate produces the artifacts these regimes demand, but compliance is a human job. Counsel reads the bundle; they do not run the runtime.
  • not a SaaS product Apache 2.0; ships as source. There is no managed service, no telemetry, no call-home. Run it on a hospital GPU node, run it on a developer laptop, run it on a regulator's air-gapped workstation.
  • not microsoft/asclepius Microsoft's Asclepius is a sample app: Azure OpenAI generating patient-friendly summaries of lab results, .NET Blazor + Python Functions + Semantic Kernel. It is exactly the kind of clinical-AI tool our substrate is built to wrap. Different layer, same name. See ADR-012.
  • not starmpcc/Asclepius The Korean clinical LLM (7B/13B/Llama3-8B/Mistral) trained on synthetic discharge summaries. It is a model. We are the runtime that audits any model — including that one. The substrate sits below the weights, not beside them.
  • not Asclepius-Med/Asclepius The ACL 2025 multi-modal medical evaluation benchmark (15 specialties, 3 categories, 8 sub-tasks). It is a dataset and a leaderboard. Our EvaluationHarness can drive a wrapped model through it; the benchmark sits on top of the substrate, not in place of it.
  • not asclepius-network A dormant Polkadot/Substrate healthcare DAO. We are not a blockchain. Our Merkle ledger is a signed local append-only log — no consensus protocol, no token, no chain reorgs.
  • not the Dicoding student app The Dicoding "Belajar Penerapan Machine Learning untuk Android" course ships an Asclepius-named skin-cancer detection app as the capstone; ~50 student forks exist on GitHub. None of them are us; we are not Android, not skin-cancer-specific, and not coursework.

If a feature would push the project toward one of those — making Asclepius into the scribe, into the dashboard, into the EHR plugin — we push back. Substrate, not app. The whole point is to be the layer underneath, where everyone agrees the bytes mean what they say.
