§01 · bytes · cpu · disk · honest numbers

What does it actually cost?

Substrate-grade software should fit on the budget side of a hospital's ledger, not feel like a separate one. Below: bytes per entry, entries per day for typical deployments, signing CPU, verification CPU, bundle export size, retention multiplier — with worked examples for small, medium, and large operators. Round numbers, conservative assumptions, verifiable arithmetic.

§ 01 · per-entry unit costs

One signed entry.

Every clinical-AI inference produces exactly one ledger entry. Optional related entries (consent gate, drift snapshot, key-rotation event) are rare relative to inference volume. The unit cost of "an entry" is what sets every other line below.

canonical-JSON bytes ≈ 480 BMedian observed in production traces. P99 ≈ 720 B.

§ 03 · evidence-bundle costs

Bundle exports — the regulator side.

Bundles are the artefact handed to auditors. They are USTAR tar files with a manifest, a JSONL ledger excerpt, drift snapshots, calibration data, and a public key. Their size depends on what is included; their build time is dominated by reading the entries and re-hashing them.

scopetypical entriesbundle sizebuild time
single encounter10–40 ≈ 80 KB < 50 ms
single patient · 1 yr~1 k ≈ 1.5 MB < 200 ms
department · 1 month~150 k≈ 90 MB ≈ 4 s
whole system · 1 year~7 M ≈ 4 GB ~3 min
+ post-quantum dual-sign+ 3 KB / entry×6×1.4
 Bundles are uncompressed by default · deterministic byte-for-byte. ML-DSA dual-signing arrives in v1.0 — see ADR-010.
§ 04 · retention

How long we keep things.

Retention is set by the regulatory framework the operator works under, not by the substrate. The substrate is append-only, so deletion is a deliberate act. The defaults below match HIPAA + EU AI Act + Joint Commission and align with what most clinical operators end up choosing.

artefactdefaultrationale
ledger entries (all kinds) 7 yearsStandard medical-record retention floor across the US.
audio / raw input 7 days Hashed and bound to the entry within seconds; raw bytes do not persist.
drift snapshots 2 yearsLong enough for FDA post-market reporting; can be re-derived from entries if needed.
consent grants/revocations indefiniteRequired to demonstrate authority for every past action.
signing keys (public) indefiniteVerifiers must be able to check old signatures forever.
signing keys (private) archivedCold custody after rotation. Never deleted — see PB-02.
evidence bundles case-drivenBuilt on demand. The chain is the source of truth.

Why we don't compress the ledger. Compression would make auditor-tooling reach harder for entries; the cost of uncompressed storage at substrate scale (4–350 GB/yr) is rounding error on the budget of any operator running clinical AI. Optimise the audit, not the bytes.

§ 05 · what an audit costs without us

The counterfactual.

Substrate-grade audit machinery shifts where work happens. The work that used to land on legal, compliance, and SRE during an audit, post-mortem, or peer-review case is now done up-front, automatically, by the runtime. A few illustrative comparisons:

scenariowithout substratewith Asclepius
peer-review of a flagged decision Legal pulls EHR, vendor support is contacted, screenshots are reconstructed. Days–weeks. asclepius bundle export --encounter ENC-… · single signed artefact · minutes.
FDA post-market reporting Annual aggregation across systems, manual sample selection. Quarter-long cycle. Aggregate signed entries continuously; the report writes itself from the chain.
subpoena response Custodian-of-records affidavit, screenshots, per-system exports, IT testimony. Bundle export + verifier binary handed to legal — see PB-06.
model rollback after a regression Identify affected encounters by SQL queries against EHR plus model logs that may not match. Filter on weights_sha256 in the chain; affected rows are deterministic.
privacy-revocation cascade Manual sweep, often missed; revocation propagation has no SLA. Registry refuses subsequent checks within seconds; affected rows annotated by export — PB-05.

The honest framing. Asclepius does not save the operator the cost of their auditor's hourly rate. It shifts the auditor's hour from "reconstruct what happened" to "verify what is recorded." That is a quantitatively different audit; it is also the only kind a regulator can rely on.

The substrate is not free.

Nothing is free. The substrate trades a small, predictable, durable cost — a fraction of a CPU core, a few gigabytes per year, an additional operational discipline — for an enormous reduction in catastrophic-tail risk. Operators who run clinical AI without it are also paying — they just don't know what for, until the regulator asks them.
